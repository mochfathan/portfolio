Conceptual framework — Markowitz & CAPM
1 · Expected Return — CAPM Formula
E(Rᵢ) = Rƒ + βᵢ × [E(Rₘ) − Rƒ]
Developed by William Sharpe (1964), John Lintner (1965), and Jan Mossin (1966), CAPM states that the expected return of any asset equals the risk-free rate plus a premium proportional to the asset's systematic risk (β). The equity risk premium [E(Rₘ) − Rƒ] represents the additional return investors demand for accepting market-level risk rather than holding a risk-free asset.

Portfolio Expected Return is simply the weighted average: E(Rₚ) = wA·E(RA) + wB·E(RB). This is linear in weights — no diversification effect on expected return, only on risk.
2 · Portfolio Variance — Markowitz (1952)
σ²ₚ = w²A·σ²A + w²B·σ²B + 2·wA·wB·σA·σB·ρAB
Harry Markowitz proved in 1952 that portfolio variance is not simply the weighted average of individual variances — the covariance term 2·wA·wB·σA·σB·ρ drives the key insight: whenever ρ < 1, portfolio risk is strictly less than the weighted average of individual risks. This is the mathematical proof of diversification. The minimum portfolio variance is achieved when ρ = −1 (perfect inverse correlation).
3 · Beta (β) — Systematic Risk Measure
β = Cov(Rᵢ, Rₘ) / Var(Rₘ) = ρᵢₘ · (σᵢ / σₘ)
Beta measures how much an asset moves relative to the market portfolio. β = 1 means the asset moves exactly with the market; β < 1 is defensive (less volatile); β > 1 is aggressive (more volatile). R² (coefficient of determination) = β² × (σₘ/σᵢ)² tells you what fraction of a stock's total variance is explained by market movements — the rest is idiosyncratic (firm-specific).
4 · Systematic vs. Unsystematic Risk
Systematic (Non-diversifiable)
Macro factors affecting all stocks: interest rates, inflation, currency crises, geopolitical events, recession. Cannot be eliminated by diversification. Measured by β. Compensated by market risk premium.
Unsystematic (Diversifiable)
Firm-specific events: earnings surprises, management changes, product failures, sector cycles. Can be reduced by holding 20–30+ uncorrelated stocks. Not compensated in CAPM (investors expected to diversify it away).
5 · Sharpe & Treynor Ratios
Sharpe = (E(Rₚ) − Rƒ) / σₚ   |   Treynor = (E(Rₚ) − Rƒ) / βₚ
Sharpe Ratio (William Sharpe, 1966): risk-adjusted return per unit of total risk (σ). Useful for evaluating standalone portfolio quality. Higher is better.
Treynor Ratio (Jack Treynor, 1965): risk-adjusted return per unit of systematic risk (β). Useful when comparing portfolios held within a larger diversified context — it ignores idiosyncratic risk since that can be diversified away.
6 · Limitations of CAPM
Fama & French (1992, 1993) showed empirically that CAPM under-explains cross-sectional returns — size (SMB) and value (HML) factors matter beyond β. Correlation instability (Longin & Solnik, 2001): ρ tends to increase toward 1 during market crises, precisely when diversification is most needed — the "correlation breakdown" problem. CAPM also assumes normal return distributions, a single-period investment horizon, frictionless markets, and homogeneous investor expectations — all stylised simplifications that hold imperfectly in real markets.
